You are given a debate topic, background information, and an argument from that debate topic. Your task is to analyze the argument and determine whether it contains a formal fallacy, an informal fallacy, both, or neither.

## Definitions

**Formal Fallacy**: An argument with a flaw in its logical structure. The argument has an invalid logical form, making it deductively unsound regardless of the truth of its premises. Examples include (but not limited to):
- Affirming the consequent
- Denying the antecedent
- Undistributed middle
- Illicit major/minor
- Affirming a disjunct
- Denying a conjunct
- Quantifier shift
- Existential fallacy
- Fallacy of the inverse/converse

**Informal Fallacy**: An argument with a flaw in its content, context, or reasoning process rather than its logical structure. The error lies in relevance or ambiguity. Examples include (but not limited to):
- Ad hominem
- Straw man
- Slippery slope
- Appeal to authority/emotion/ignorance
- False dilemma
- Hasty generalization
- Post hoc ergo propter hoc
- Red herring
- Tu quoque
- Bandwagon
- Loaded language
- Oversimplification

## Instructions

1. Carefully read the argument in context of the debate topic and background.
2. Identify the premises and conclusion of the argument.
3. Check if the logical form/structure is valid (formal analysis).
4. Check if the content and reasoning have some flaw (informal analysis).
- Flag an informal fallacy ONLY if the flaw is CRITICAL. Consider the following:
  - Not every imperfection is a fallacy. Real-world arguments may rely on inductive reasoning, which by nature does not guarantee certainty. This alone does not constitute a fallacy. For example, If the argument provides multiple relevant examples or credible data points that reasonably support the conclusion, it should NOT be flagged as hasty generalization.
  - Apply the principle of charity. Interpret the argument in its strongest reasonable form before judging it as fallacious.
